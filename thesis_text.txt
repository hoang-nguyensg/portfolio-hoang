Definition and Acronyms
Acronym Definition
AI Artificial Intelligence — the field of computer science that de-
velops systems capable of performing human-like cognitive
tasks such as perception, reasoning, learning, and decision-
making.
DL Deep Learning — a subfield of machine learning that uses deep
neural networks with many layers to automatically learn hier-
archical feature representations from large datasets.
PM Project Manager — the individual responsible for planning, ex-
ecuting, and overseeing project activities to ensure objectives
regarding scope, schedule, and cost are met.
PMP Project Management Plan — a structured document outlining
how a project will be executed, monitored, and controlled, in-
cluding scope, timeline, resources, risks, and communication.
WBS Work Breakdown Structure — a hierarchical decomposition of
project deliverables into smaller, manageable work packages
used for planning and tracking.
VLM Vision-Language Model — a multimodal architecture capable
of jointly processing visual and textual data for tasks such as
captioning, VQA, or cross-modal retrieval.
CXR Chest X-Ray — a radiographic imaging technique used to ana-
lyze thoracic structures, including lungs, heart, and chest cav-
ity.
BLEU Bilingual Evaluation Understudy — a metric for evaluating ma-
chine translation quality based on n-gram precision matching
between generated and reference text.
ROUGE Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation — a family
of metrics for evaluating summarization or generation tasks by
measuring n-gram or sequence overlap with reference text, em-
phasizing recall.
NLP Natural Language Processing — an AI domain focused on en-
abling machines to understand, interpret, and generate human
language.

Acronym Definition
GPU Graphics Processing Unit — a highly parallel processor opti-
mized for performing large-scale matrix operations, enabling
fast training and inference of deep learning models.
ViT Vision Transformer — an attention-based neural architecture
that processes an image as a sequence of patches, enabling
global context modeling without convolution.
API Application Programming Interface — a set of definitions and
protocols that enable different software components or sys-
tems to communicate programmatically.
LLM Large Language Model — a neural model with billions of pa-
rameters trained on massive corpora to perform advanced lan-
guage understanding and generation tasks.
Findings The descriptive observations or results extracted from medical
imaging or reports—usually indicating abnormalities, patterns,
or notable characteristics.
Impressions A clinician’s summarized interpretation of findings, often rep-
resenting the diagnostic conclusion or most likely condition.
W&B Weights & Biases — a machine learning experiment tracking
platform used to log metrics, visualize training, and manage
model versions.
Qwen A family of large language and multimodal models developed
by Alibaba, optimized for reasoning, vision-language tasks, and
efficient deployment.
MiniCPM-V A lightweight Vision-Language Model designed for efficient in-
ference on limited hardware while maintaining strong perfor-
mance.
DeepSpeed A deep learning optimization library by Microsoft provid-
ing distributed training, memory efficiency, and speedups for
large-scale models.

Acronym Definition
CPU Central Processing Unit — the primary general-purpose pro-
cessor of a computer that handles sequential tasks and system
operations.
Dr. Benchmark (Doctor Benchmark) — a benchmarking suite used to evaluate
medical AI models on tasks such as diagnosis, reasoning, and
multimodal understanding.
AdamW An improved variant of the Adam optimizer that decouples
weight decay from gradient updates, enhancing generalization
and convergence stability.
Cosine Cosine Annealing Learning Rate — a learning rate schedule that
follows a cosine decay curve to improve training dynamics.
BF16 Brain Floating Point 16-bit — a numerical precision format of-
fering faster computation and lower memory use than FP32
while maintaining stable gradients.
API Application Programming Interface — a structured set of pro-
tocols enabling communication between software systems or
models.
Gradio A Python framework for building quick, interactive web inter-
faces for machine learning models.
vLLM A high-performance inference engine optimized for serving
large language models efficiently using memory-saving tech-
niques like PagedAttention.

2.1 Project Overview
In modern healthcare, Chest X-ray (CXR) imaging remains one of the most widely used diagnostic
methods globally. It serves as a frontline technique for evaluating diseases related to the lungs, heart.
While the imaging process itself has been automated and standardized for many years, the most
critical stage—interpreting, analyzing, and generating diagnostic reports—still relies entirely on ra-
diologists. This task requires intense concentration, extensive clinical experience, and considerable
processing time, especially in hospitals that receive a large number of patients daily.
In Vietnam, this strain is even more pronounced as many healthcare facilities, particularly at the
provincial and district levels, face a shortage of highly specialized radiologists. The high workload of
reading hundreds of chest X-rays per day increases fatigue, inconsistency in report descriptions, and
the risk of diagnostic errors. These challenges not only affect the quality of radiological reports but
also slow down the entire patient care workflow.
Addressing this urgent need, this project aims to develop a Vision–Language AI system capable of
automatically generating Vietnamese medical captions for chest X-ray images. The system focuses on
identifying and describing important anatomical structures—such as lungs, heart, diaphragm, and
pleura—and producing observations, Findings, and Impressions following standard clinical report
formats. The goal is not to replace radiologists but to serve as an assistive tool that reduces workload,
enhances linguistic consistency, shortens processing time, and improves diagnostic accuracy.
A notable strength of the project lies in the quality of its training data. Most large chest X-ray datasets
worldwide are collected from Western patients, who differ from Vietnamese patients in body habitus,
anatomical structure, and disease patterns. This difference limits the applicability of international
AI models in Vietnam’s clinical environment. Recognizing this, the project obtained a dedicated X-
ray dataset from Military Hospital 175, ensuring anatomical, pathological, linguistic, and contextual
suitability for Vietnamese healthcare. This is a crucial factor in enabling the AI system to generate
accurate and clinically relevant reports.
By applying advanced Vision–Language architectures, the system can not only analyze medical im-
ages but also articulate clinical Findings in natural, domain-appropriate Vietnamese. The final prod-
uct is designed to support radiologists in tasks such as rapid abnormality screening, drafting stan-
dardized reports, and providing initial clinical Impressions—thereby improving hospital workflow
efficiency.
Beyond practical applications, the project holds significant research value by pioneering the devel-
opment of multimodal AI for healthcare in Vietnam. Its success may lay the foundation for similar AI
systems for CT, MRI, and ultrasound imaging, contributing to digital transformation in the medical
sector.

2.2 Background
In Vietnam, although no comprehensive national statistics are publicly available, the Ministry of
Health and various clinical reports consistently highlight that “a shortage of radiologists and in-
sufficient X-ray report descriptions” remains a major bottleneck at provincial and district hospi-
tals—where each radiologist must handle a high volume of daily cases, particularly chest X-rays and
trauma films. Despite being essential for diagnosing pulmonary, cardiac, and thoracic conditions, X-
ray interpretation demands specialized skills and significant time investment, placing heavy pressure
on the medical workforce.
Additionally, most existing global chest X-ray datasets were collected from non-Vietnamese popula-
tions whose anatomical and physiological characteristics differ from Vietnamese patients. As a re-
sult, they cannot be directly applied to Vietnam’s clinical settings. Moreover, Vietnam currently lacks
a standardized, large-scale medical X-ray dataset for AI research on Vietnamese subjects. For this rea-
son, the project team acquired a dedicated dataset from Military Hospital 175 to ensure biological,
anatomical, and linguistic compatibility for training a Vietnamese medical captioning system.
Overall, the combination of increasing clinical demand, workforce shortages, and the massive vol-
ume of X-rays creates a substantial gap in healthcare systems worldwide—especially in developing
countries like Vietnam. In this context, modern Vision–Language models trained on large, high-
quality datasets present a promising approach for automated radiology report generation. Although
further real-world clinical evaluation is needed, these models show clear potential in reducing work-
load, minimizing errors, and improving healthcare quality in resource-limited settings, providing a
strong foundation for future medical AI development.
2.3 Objective
This project applies artificial intelligence—specifically Vietnamese-supported Vision–Language Mod-
els (VLM)—to assist radiologists in interpreting chest X-rays more quickly and accurately. The objec-
tive is to support diagnostic reasoning and clinical documentation while reducing the workload for
physicians, particularly in high-pressure and understaffed medical environments.
The dataset provided by Military Hospital 175 contains chest X-ray images paired with professional
medical reports, serving as the foundation for building a specialized medical captioning system.
The primary goal is to develop an automated system capable of generating detailed, clinically ap-
propriate Vietnamese descriptions for chest X-rays. The system aims to provide accurate Findings
and diagnostic Impressions that assist radiologists in rapidly evaluating images, thereby shortening
diagnostic turnaround times.
The system addresses the problem of generating detailed Findings and clinical Impressions from
chest X-ray images. It operates in three main stages: (1) Generate Findings from the input image, (2)
Generate Impressions based on both the image and the Findings, (3) Support multi-turn interactions
that combine visual and linguistic cues. Accuracy and consistency of input/output formatting are
key factors in effective model training.
This system has the potential to reduce physician workload, enhance diagnostic accuracy, and im-
prove patient care. From a research perspective, the project advances Vietnamese-compatible AI
models in healthcare and contributes to the development of multimodal medical AI systems.

2.4 Problem Statement
Current chest X-ray interpretation workflows in Vietnam and many other countries face several major
challenges.
Overloaded workforce and risk of diagnostic errors. Radiologists must read large numbers of X-rays
daily, especially at provincial/district hospitals, leading to fatigue, reduced accuracy, and increased
risk of missing critical abnormalities. High workload not only raises the likelihood of errors but also
makes it difficult for radiologists to maintain consistent, high-quality clinical reporting.
Lack of standardization in medical reporting. X-ray descriptions are currently inconsistent, creating
challenges for data storage, retrieval, analysis, and integration into digital health systems. Variations
in experience and writing style lead to non-uniform reports, affecting the evaluation and comparison
of clinical data.
Shortage of experienced radiologists at lower-tier hospitals. Provincial and district healthcare cen-
ters often lack highly specialized radiologists, resulting in: (1) Delayed diagnosis and treatment, (2)
Inaccurate conclusions, (3) Reduced quality of patient care.
Limitations of existing AI systems. Most current medical AI models focus only on classification or
abnormality detection and cannot generate full Vietnamese clinical reports. Few systems can repli-
cate a radiologist’s ability to "see – interpret – articulate" while maintaining consistency, accuracy,
and speed.
Need for Vietnamese-language support. Most existing models are trained on English-language datasets
and cannot be directly applied to Vietnamese clinical workflows. A dedicated system is needed to un-
derstand and generate Vietnamese medical descriptions consistent with local clinical terminology
and reporting standards.
In summary, there is an urgent need for an AI system capable of automatically generating detailed
Findings and clinical Impressions for chest X-rays in Vietnamese—supporting radiologists in reduc-
ing workload, improving accuracy, and standardizing medical reporting.
Such a system must integrate strong visual recognition with accurate medical language generation,
ensuring fast, reliable, and clinically meaningful outputs suitable for deployment in Vietnam.

2.6 Project Scope & Limitations
This section defines the boundaries of the project, clarifying what is included and what lies beyond
the current scope, as well as acknowledging technical and practical constraints.
2.6.1 Project Scope
Primary Focus: Chest X-ray Report Generation
The project specifically targets chest X-ray (CXR) images and focuses on generating two main com-
ponents of radiological reports:
•Findings: Detailed descriptions of anatomical structures and observed abnormalities (e.g.,
lung opacities, heart size, pleural effusion).
•Impressions: Clinical summaries and diagnostic conclusions based on the Findings.
Vietnamese Language Support
The system is designed to generate medical reports exclusively in Vietnamese, following clinical ter-
minology and reporting standards used in Vietnamese hospitals. All training data, model outputs,
and evaluation metrics are aligned with Vietnamese medical language conventions.
Dataset and Clinical Context
The project uses a proprietary dataset provided by Military Hospital 175, comprising chest X-ray im-
ages paired with professional Vietnamese radiology reports. This ensures that the AI system is trained
on data representative of Vietnamese patients’ anatomical characteristics, disease patterns, and clin-
ical workflows.
Multi-Stage Training Approach
The system employs a three-stage fine-tuning strategy:
•Stage 1:Training on Findings generation
•Stage 2:Training on Impressions generation
•Stage 3:Training on multi-turn generation interactions combining visual and textual inputs
Model Comparison and Selection
Six state-of-the-art Vision–Language Models (VLMs) are trained and evaluated under identical con-
ditions to identify the most suitable architecture for Vietnamese chest X-ray captioning: Vintern-1B-
7
v3.5 [1], InternVL2.5-1B [2], Qwen2-VL-2B [3], Qwen2-VL-7B [3], LaVy-8B [4], and MiniCPM-V-2.6-
8B [5].
Target Users
The system is intended as an assistive tool for:
• Radiologists and physicians in Vietnamese hospitals
• Healthcare facilities facing radiologist shortages or high imaging volumes
• Medical institutions seeking to standardize radiology reporting workflows
Deployment Environment:The project aims to develop a proof-of-concept system suitable for inte-
gration into hospital PACS (Picture Archiving and Communication System) or standalone diagnostic
workstations. Real-world clinical deployment and regulatory approval are outside the current scope
but represent future objectives.
2.6.2 Limitations
Image Modality Restriction:The system is limited to chest X-ray images only. It does not support
other radiological modalities such as CT, MRI, ultrasound, or mammography. Extension to other
imaging types would require additional datasets and model retraining.
Frontal View Only:The current implementation primarily focuses on frontal (PA/AP) chest X-rays.
Lateral views and specialized projections are not explicitly modeled, though they may be included in
future iterations.
Single Language Support:The system generates reports exclusively in Vietnamese. It does not sup-
port multilingual outputs or real-time translation to other languages. Adapting the system to English
or other languages would require re-annotation and model fine-tuning.
Dataset Size and Diversity:While the dataset from Military Hospital 175 is clinically relevant, it rep-
resents a single institution’s patient population and imaging protocols. The model’s generalization to
other hospitals, imaging equipment, or geographic regions may vary and requires further validation.
Model Interpretability and Explainability:Although the system generates textual descriptions, it does
not provide explicit visual attention maps or diagnostic reasoning explanations. Understandingwhy
the model produces certain outputs remains a challenge inherent to deep learning approaches.
Clinical Validation and Regulatory Approval:The project focuses on technical development and
evaluation using standard NLP and medical imaging metrics. Formal clinical trials, inter-rater re-
liability studies with radiologists, and regulatory approval processes (e.g., FDA, CE marking, or Viet-
nam’s Ministry of Health certification) are beyond the current scope.
Not a Standalone Diagnostic Tool:The system is designed as anassistivetool to support radiologists,
not as a replacement for human expertise. Final diagnostic decisions must always be made by qual-
ified medical professionals. The AI-generated reports serve as drafts or second opinions to enhance
efficiency and consistency.
Computational Requirements:Training and inference require substantial computational resources
(GPUs with sufficient VRAM). Deployment in resource-constrained environments may necessitate
model quantization, distillation, or cloud-based inference solutions.
Handling of Rare or Complex Cases:The model is trained on common chest X-ray Findings and
8
may underperform on rare pathologies, uncommon anatomical variations, or highly complex multi-
pathology cases that are underrepresented in the training data.
Temporal and Longitudinal Analysis:The current system analyzes individual X-ray images in isola-
tion. It does not perform temporal comparisons (e.g., comparing current and prior studies) or track
disease progression over time, which are important clinical capabilities.

3 Related Work
3.1 Monolingual Vietnamese Vision-Language Models
Vintern-1B-v3.5[1] is a 1-billion-parameter multimodal large language model (MLLM) tailored for
Vietnamese language tasks. Recent years have seen rapid progress in multimodal large language
models (MLLMs), with closed-source systems like GPT-4o and Gemini Pro Vision achieving highly
capable cross-modal reasoning. Open-source efforts have also accelerated, led by models such as the
InternVL series, which align visual inputs with LLMs through visual instruction tuning. InternVL2-1B,
built on the distilled InternViT-300M encoder and paired with Qwen2-0.5B-Instruct, delivers strong
multilingual performance, including excellent Vietnamese understanding. For Vietnamese MLLMs,
models like V-Vistral and LaVy mark important progress, yet significant challenges remain in docu-
ment understanding, charts, infographics, and Vietnamese scene text.
LaVy-8B[4] is a Vietnamese multimodal large language model built upon the Vistral-7B language
model and the CLIP-Large visual encoder. Renabled strong performance in tasks like dialogue, cre-
ative writing, and problem-solving, with models such as LLaMA, Mistral, and Gemma becoming
foundation models. Vietnamese models, such as PhoGPT and Vistral, show promising results on
language-specific benchmarks. Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) extend these capabilities to text, im-
ages, and other modalities. Closed-source models like GPT-4 and Gemini Pro Vision demonstrate
strong visual-language performance, while open-source models such as Flamingo, BLIP-2, LLaVA,
and MiniGPT-4 use techniques like gated attention and visual instruction tuning to align visual in-
puts with LLMs. Research also explores lightweight MLLMs like Bunny and vision-language tasks for
low-resource languages, reflecting growing interest in scalable multimodal reasoning.
3.2 Multilingual Vision-Language Models
InternVL2.5-1B[6] is an advanced MLLM that builds upon the InternVL 2.0 architecture, introduc-
ing significant enhancements in training strategies and data quality. Computer vision has progressed
from CNNs to Vision Transformers, but most vision models remain visual-only and relatively small,
limiting their use in multimodal systems. In contrast, LLMs like GPT-4, LLaMA, and Qwen have
rapidly advanced NLP and driven interest in multimodal interaction. This convergence produced
VLLMs such as Flamingo, LLaVA, MiniGPT-4, KOSMOS-2, and Qwen-VL, which integrate visual un-
derstanding through instruction tuning and grounding. Although multimodal models are improving
quickly, vision and vision-language foundation models still lag behind LLMs, leaving key challenges
for future development.
Qwen2-VL-2B-Instruct[3] andQwen2-VL-7B-Instruct[3] are part of the Qwen2-VL series.Qwen2-
VL advances the Qwen-VL family with a dynamic-resolution mechanism that converts images into
flexible visual tokens, allowing the model to keep fine details while remaining efficient. It also intro-
duces M-RoPE, a multidimensional positional embedding for unified reasoning across text, images,
and videos. Available in 2B–7B sizes, Qwen2-VL follows the design direction of models like Kosmos-2
and PaLI-X, emphasising adaptability to diverse inputs. Overall, it represents a modern, scalable ap-
proach to multimodal encoding with strong potential for OCR, high-resolution image understanding,
and video tasks.

MiniCPM-V-2.6-8B[5] is the latest and most capable model in the MiniCPM-V series, comprising 8
billion parameters. Built on SigLip-400M and Qwen2-7B architectures, it supports the processing of
single images, multiple images, and videos. MiniCPM-V-2.6-8B builds on the trend of lightweight Vi-
sion–Language Models optimised for efficiency, following prior work on compact multimodal archi-
tectures that balance visual understanding and language generation. Existing studies on MiniCPM-V
primarily emphasize improved alignment, better instruction tuning, and reduced inference cost, po-
sitioning it as a practical VLM for real-world deployment, especially where computational resources
are limited

ViX-Ray was collected from examination records of patients at Military Medical Hospital 175, com-

4.1.3 Data Preprocessing
Figure 5: Comprehensive data preprocessing pipeline illustrating the systematic workflow from raw
data acquisition through parallel text and image preprocessing streams, hierarchical three-stage an-
notation architecture, and final stratified dataset partitioning into training, development, and test
subsets
The data preprocessing phase constitutes a critical foundation for ensuring data quality and model
performance in our automated radiology report generation system. Operating on the Military Hospi-
tal 175 dataset comprising 137,930 chest X-ray examinations, we implement a comprehensive dual-
pipeline preprocessing framework that addresses the heterogeneous nature of multimodal medical
data, as depicted in Figure 5.
Textual Data Processing Pipeline.The clinical text preprocessing pipeline incorporates multiple
normalization steps to mitigate noise and standardize linguistic representations. We systematically
remove punctuation marks and special characters that introduce unnecessary lexical variability, while
employing stop word elimination to reduce dimensionality without sacrificing semantic content.
This preprocessing strategy ensures that subsequent natural language processing (NLP) components
can effectively capture clinically relevant patterns while maintaining computational efficiency.
Medical Imaging Processing Pipeline.For radiographic images originally stored in DICOM (Digital
Imaging and Communications in Medicine) format, we perform lossless conversion to PNG format
to ensure compatibility with modern deep learning frameworks while preserving critical diagnostic
information. This conversion maintains the original pixel intensity distributions and spatial resolu-
tions necessary for accurate feature extraction by VLMs.
Three-Stage Annotation Architecture.The preprocessed multimodal data is subsequently organized
into a hierarchical three-stage annotation framework that mirrors the clinical reasoning process of
radiologists. Stage 1 (Findings Generation) focuses on extracting objective observations from radio-
graphic images paired with patient clinical information. Stage 2 (Impressions Generation) synthe-
sizes these Findings into diagnostic interpretations, incorporating domain knowledge and clinical
13
context. Stage 3 (Multi-turn Generation) enables the complete report generation workflow, combin-
ing both Findings and Impressions in a coherent narrative structure that facilitates iterative refine-
ment and multi-turn dialogue capabilities.
Dataset Partitioning Strategy.To ensure robust model evaluation and generalization, we partition
the complete preprocessed dataset into two primary subsets with distinct characteristics. Dataset 1
contains 5,400 carefully curated samples and is divided into training (4,320 samples, 80%), develop-
ment (540 samples, 10%), and test (540 samples, 10%) sets. Dataset 2, comprising 137,930 samples,
follows a similar partitioning scheme with 110,344 training samples (80%), 13,793 development sam-
ples (10%), and 13,793 test samples (10%). This stratified splitting approach maintains distributional
consistency across subsets while providing sufficient data for model training, hyperparameter tun-
ing, and unbiased performance assessment.

4.2.2 Training Setup and Infrastructure
All experiments are conducted using thesftrainframework, selected for its flexibility and efficiency
in scaling large VLMs across distributed computing environments. To balance detail preservation
with computational constraints, all input images are uniformly resized to 448×448 pixels prior to
training—a resolution that maintains clinically relevant anatomical features while fitting within GPU
memory limitations.
Memory Challenges in Large Model Training.The training of large-scale VLMs presents substan-
tial memory management challenges that vary dramatically with model size. For relatively compact
models such as our 1B and 2B parameter variants, full fine-tuning—wherein all parameters of both
the vision encoder and language decoder are simultaneously updated—can be performed without
specialized memory optimization techniques on standard GPU configurations.
However, scaling to larger architectures introduces critical memory bottlenecks. Figure 15 illustrates
a representative out-of-memory (OOM) error encountered during initial training attempts of our 7B
parameter model on a multi-GPU system with 7×5090 GPUs (totaling approximately 224GB aggregate
VRAM). The error traceback reveals that PyTorch’s default memory allocation strategy exhausts avail-
able GPU memory during the forward pass, with the specific failure occurring at 15.45 GiB allocated
on GPU 4 (which has 31.36 GiB total capacity) when attempting to allocate an additional 16.48 GiB
for intermediate activations. This memory pressure stems from multiple simultaneous demands:
•Model Parameters: 7B-8B parameter models in FP32 require 28-32 GB for weights alone; even
with BF16 precision, this reduces only to 14-16 GB per GPU
•Optimizer States: AdamW maintains two additional states (momentum and variance) per pa-
rameter, tripling memory requirements to 42-48 GB for FP32 or 21-24 GB for BF16
•Gradients: Backpropagation necessitates storing gradients for all parameters, adding another
14-16 GB (BF16)
•Activations: Forward pass activations for high-resolution medical images (448×448) with deep
vision encoders generate substantial intermediate tensors, often exceeding 10-15 GB per batch
24
These cumulative memory demands far exceed single-GPU capacity and even challenge naive multi-
GPU data parallelism approaches where each GPU maintains a full model replica.
Figure 15: Out-of-memory error encountered during training of 7B parameter model without mem-
ory optimization strategies. The error occurs when PyTorch attempts to allocate 16.48 GiB for acti-
vations on GPU 4, which already has 15.45 GiB allocated, exceeding available capacity and triggering
training failure.
DeepSpeed Integration for Memory-Efficient Training.To overcome these fundamental memory
limitations while maintaining full fine-tuning capabilities, we integrate Microsoft DeepSpeed [8]—a
distributed training optimization library specifically designed for large-scale model training. Deep-
Speed’s Zero Redundancy Optimizer (ZeRO) [8] provides multiple stages of memory optimization
through intelligent partitioning of model states across GPUs:
•ZeRO Stage 3: Extends partitioning to model parameters themselves, distributing the complete
model state (parameters, gradients, and optimizer states) across all available GPUs. During for-
ward and backward passes, required parameters are fetched on-demand via high-bandwidth
GPU interconnects (NVLink/NVSwitch), enabling training of models that would otherwise be
impossible to fit on available hardware. This approach reduces per-GPU memory to approxi-
mately 1/Nof the total model size, with communication overhead minimized through Deep-
Speed’s optimized collective operations.
•CPU Offloading: For particularly memory-constrained scenarios, we enable offloading of op-
timizer states and parameters to CPU RAM during periods when they are not actively required
for computation. While this introduces CPU-GPU data transfer overhead, the substantially
larger CPU memory capacity (typically 256-512 GB on our training nodes) provides headroom
for models that would otherwise trigger OOM errors even with ZeRO partitioning.
•Gradient Checkpointing Integration: DeepSpeed seamlessly integrates with gradient check-
pointing (activation recomputation), enabling hybrid memory-computation trade-offs. By se-
lectively recomputing intermediate activations during backpropagation rather than storing them
25
throughout the forward pass, we reduce activation memory by 70-80% at the cost of approx-
imately 30% additional computation time—an acceptable trade-off for enabling training of
larger models.
Figure 16 demonstrates successful training execution after DeepSpeed integration, with the same 7B
parameter model now training stably across 7 GPUs. The process monitoring panel shows balanced
GPU memory utilization (approximately 50-56% across all devices) with no OOM errors, confirming
that ZeRO’s memory optimizations successfully distribute the model state. The training logs indicate
smooth progression through multiple training steps with consistent gradient computations, validat-
ing that the optimizations preserve training dynamics while resolving memory constraints.
Figure 16: Successful training execution of 7B parameter model after DeepSpeed integration with
ZeRO Stage 3 and CPU offloading. GPU utilization remains stable at 50-56% with no memory errors,
demonstrating effective distribution of model states across 7 5090 GPUs. Training proceeds smoothly
with consistent gradient computations and loss convergence.
Full Fine-Tuning Philosophy.Despite the additional complexity introduced by distributed train-
ing optimizations, we deliberately maintain a full fine-tuning approach—updating all parameters in
both vision encoders and language decoders—rather than adopting parameter-efficient fine-tuning
(PEFT) methods such as LoRA [9] or adapters. This decision reflects several considerations:
1.Domain Shift Magnitude: Medical radiology represents a substantial domain shift from gen-
eral vision-language tasks. Full fine-tuning enables comprehensive adaptation of visual feature
extractors to medical imaging characteristics (tissue contrast, pathological patterns, anatomi-
cal structures) and language models to specialized medical terminology and reporting conven-
tions.
2.Comparative Evaluation: Maintaining consistent full fine-tuning across all model sizes en-
ables fair comparison of architectural choices and model scale effects, isolated from confound-
26
ing factors introduced by different adaptation strategies.
This infrastructure design—combining advanced memory optimizations with full parameter updates—enables
rigorous evaluation of model architectures and scale effects under realistic resource constraints, pro-
viding insights into both performance and practical deployability of large VLMs for clinical radiology
applications.

superior capability in generating clinically accurate and linguistically coherent radiology reports. The
model’s performance advantage is particularly pronounced in ROUGE-L and BLEU, indicating strong

innovations beneficial for medical imaging:
•Dynamic Resolution Vision Encoder: Unlike fixed-resolution encoders in competing architec-

Having identified Qwen2-VL-7B as the optimal architecture, we proceed to full-scale training on

to leverage the substantially larger dataset to improve model generalization, reduce overfitting to
specific imaging patterns, and enhance coverage of rare pathological conditions underrepresented
in the smaller subset.

model on 137,930 samples, we deploy a distributed training configuration utilizing 7×NVIDIA RTX

and combined computational throughput exceeding 1,000 TFLOPS for BF16 operations, enabling
reasonable training times while maintaining full fine-tuning without parameter-efficient approxima-
tions.
As in Phase 1, we employ DeepSpeed ZeRO Stage 3 with CPU offloading and gradient checkpointing
to overcome per-GPU memory limitations. The 7-GPU configuration enables near-linear scaling of
effective batch size through data parallelism while distributing model states to fit within individual
GPU memory constraints. High-bandwidth NVLink interconnects between GPUs minimize com-
munication overhead associated with ZeRO’s parameter gathering operations, maintaining training
efficiency despite distributed memory architecture.

Monolingual Result:The results in Stage 1 – findings generation – show that both LaVy and Vintern-
v3.5 are capable of producing observations similar to those written by radiologists, as reflected by

Multilingual Result:The results in Table 13 show that among the multilingual models—InternVL2.5,

examples in Appendix 6.1.6, impressions typically consist of 1–2 concise clinical diagnoses. Still, the

ViX-Ray dataset.
Multi-turn Generation:The results in Table 16 indicate that most models exhibited a slight decline in

VL-7B and MiniCPM-V (8B) demonstrated notable improvements across all evaluation metrics, in-
cluding lexical quality, precision, and recall. For example, Qwen2-VL-7B achieved a substantial boost
in impressions generation, with average lexical scores increasing by more than 20%, alongside ex-
pected gains in factual accuracy by more than 29% both precision and recall metrics. These findings
suggest that multi-turn training more accurately mirrors the diagnostic reasoning process of radiol-
ogists, where findings are first described before clinical conclusions are drawn, and underscore the
robustness of larger models when fine-tuned on a comprehensive Vietnamese instruction dataset.

the Qwen2VL series. For example, Qwen2VL-7B achieves an improvement of up to+31.93 BLEU,
+28.70 Precision, and+28.74 Recallon impression generation in the test set, compared to its single-
turn counterpart. These improvements are more pronounced in theImpressiontask, which benefits
from the contextual information provided by the preceding findings.
In contrast, smaller models such as Vintern-v3.5 and InternVL2.5 exhibit performance degradation
47
across nearly all metrics. For example, Vintern-v3.5 shows a drop of45.72 ROUGE-2and11.00 Re-
callin the generation of findings. This suggests that multi-turn fine-tuning may introduce additional
reasoning complexity that these lightweight models cannot effectively handle, likely due to limited
capacity and insufficient pretraining on instruction-following data. Moreover, while moderate-sized
models like Qwen2VL-2B and MiniCPM-V show partial improvements, the performance gains re-
main substantially lower than those observed in their larger counterparts. This indicates that the
effectiveness of multi-turn fine-tuning is closely tied to both model scale and the robustness of the
pretraining corpus.
Taken together, these results highlight a clear trend:multi-turn generation is not universally ben-
eficial but instead highly dependent on model capacity and pretraining richness. Larger mod-
els—most notably Qwen2VL-7B—are capable of leveraging cross-turn contextual dependencies to
produce more coherent and clinically aligned impressions. In contrast, smaller models not only fail
to capitalize on the additional interaction structure but in some cases experience performance degra-
dation, indicating that multi-turn reasoning introduces cognitive demands beyond their representa-
tional capability. These findings suggest that the advantages of multi-turn fine-tuning stem from a
model’s ability to integrate and propagate information across turns, a capability strongly tied to both
model size and the diversity of its instruction-tuning corpus. Consequently, adopting multi-turn gen-
eration may be most suitable for high-capacity models, while lighter models may require additional
architectural priors or curriculum learning strategies to effectively handle multi-step clinical reason-
ing.

6.2.1 Training Configuration
The transition from 5,400 to 137,930 training samples necessitates careful reconsideration of hyper-
parameters to ensure effective utilization of increased data while preventing optimization patholo-
gies that may arise at larger scale. Initial experiments using Phase 1’s configuration (20 epochs, batch
size 1) revealed suboptimal convergence characteristics: the model exhibited slow adaptation to the
broader distribution of imaging patterns in the expanded dataset, with validation loss plateauing
prematurely.
To address these challenges, we perform targeted hyperparameter adjustments guided by learning
dynamics analysis and validation performance monitoring. Table 20 presents the optimized config-
uration for full-scale training.

Rationale for Configuration Changes:
•Reduced Epochs (20→6): The 25.5× increase in training data provides substantially more gra-
dient updates per epoch. With 137,930 samples and batch size 15, each epoch comprises ap-
proximately 9,195 optimization steps (compared to 360 steps in Phase 1). To prevent overfitting
while ensuring sufficient exposure to the complete dataset, we reduce total epochs to 6, yield-
ing approximately 73,560 total updates—still 204× more than Phase 1 despite fewer epochs.
•Increased Batch Size (1→15): Larger batch sizes provide more stable gradient estimates, crit-
ical when training on diverse data distributions. The increase from 1 to 15 samples per de-
vice reduces gradient variance by approximately 3-4×, enabling more aggressive learning rate
schedules and faster convergence. With 7 GPUs and batch size 15, the effective global batch
size reaches 105 samples per update, providing robust statistics for parameter updates while
remaining computationally tractable.
•Cosine Schedule with Restarts: We adopt a cosine annealing schedule with periodic restarts [?
] rather than simple cosine decay. This schedule periodically "restarts" the learning rate to
higher values before decaying again, enabling the optimizer to escape local minima and ex-
plore different regions of the loss landscape. For large-scale training where suboptimal conver-
gence is a greater risk, restarts provide a mechanism for recovery without manual intervention
or hyperparameter tuning.
•Maintained Learning Rate (3×10−5): Despite batch size increases, we maintain the conserva-
tive learning rate to preserve training stability. While larger batch sizes typically permit higher
learning rates, the diversity of imaging patterns in the expanded dataset introduces additional
gradient noise that counteracts batch size benefits, justifying retention of the original learning
rate.
Training Efficiency and Convergence.The optimized configuration achieves substantially improved
training efficiency compared to naively scaling Phase 1 settings. Validation loss monitoring reveals
steady convergence throughout all 8 epochs with no signs of overfitting or gradient instabilities. The
cosine schedule with restarts enables the model to achieve lower final validation loss compared to
simple decay schedules, suggesting successful navigation of the complex loss landscape associated
with large-scale medical image-text alignment.

7 Model Deployment and Inference Optimization
The deployment phase constitutes a critical transition from model development to production-ready
systems capable of serving real-time clinical inference requests. We investigate three distinct deploy-
ment architectures, each offering different trade-offs between inference latency, memory efficiency,
and implementation complexity. This comprehensive evaluation provides empirical insights into op-
timal deployment strategies for large-scale vision-language models in clinical environments where
both response time and resource utilization are paramount considerations.
7.1 Deployment Architecture Overview
Our deployment infrastructure implements a microservices-based architecture that decouples the
presentation layer, application logic, and inference engine, enabling independent scaling and opti-
mization of each component. The system architecture comprises three primary layers: (1) Gradio
frontend interface for clinician interaction and result visualization, (2) a FastAPI-powered backend
service managing request routing, preprocessing, and response formatting, and (3) specialized infer-
ence servers executing model predictions with optimized resource utilization.
7.2 API Layer Design
7.2.1 FastAPI Implementation
We implement the API layer using FastAPI [12], a modern Python web framework optimized for build-
ing high-performance APIs with automatic OpenAPI documentation generation and native support
for asynchronous request handling. FastAPI’s Pydantic-based data validation ensures type safety and
automatic serialization/deserialization of complex medical data structures, including DICOM meta-
data, patient demographics, and multi-modal report components.
7.3 Frontend Interface
7.3.1 Gradio Integration
Gradio is a Python library that allows developers to create user interfaces for machine learning mod-
els with minimal effort. We chose Gradio for system because it provides an intuitive and user-friendly
way to interact with the system. Its ability to support various input and output types, such as images,
text, makes it highly versatile for showcasing the capabilities of system. Additionally, Gradio’s web-
based interface allows users to interact with the model directly through a browser without requiring
any additional software installation. This ensures accessibility and ease of use, making it an ideal
choice for real-time demonstrations and user testing.
7.4 Backend Inference Strategies
We evaluate three distinct model serving approaches, each leveraging different optimization tech-
niques and runtime environments. The comparative analysis encompasses inference latency, mem-
ory footprint, throughput capacity, and ease of deployment—critical factors for production medical
AI systems.
7.4.1 Native PyTorch Inference with Hugging Face Transformers
The baseline deployment approach utilizes standard PyTorch inference with models loaded directly
from the Hugging Face Model Hub. This implementation represents the most straightforward deploy-
60
ment strategy, requiring minimal infrastructure complexity and leveraging the extensive ecosystem
of pre-trained models and tokenizers available through Hugging Face Transformers [13].
Implementation Details:Models are instantiated using theAutoModelForVision2Seqclass with
automatic device mapping to available GPU resources. Image preprocessing follows the standard
pipeline defined by the model’s associated processor, ensuring consistency between training and in-
ference data transformations. Text generation employs greedy decoding with configurable tempera-
ture and top-k sampling parameters, allowing clinicians to adjust output diversity based on clinical
preferences.
Performance Characteristics:As shown in Table 27, native PyTorch inference achieves an average
latency of 22.360 seconds per report with a memory footprint of 28.1 GB VRAM. While this approach
offers maximum flexibility and ease of debugging, the substantial inference time and memory con-
sumption render it suboptimal for real-time clinical applications where sub-second response times
are increasingly expected.
7.4.2 vLLM-Accelerated Serving
To address the performance limitations of native PyTorch inference, we deploy our model using
vLLM [14], a high-throughput serving framework specifically designed for large language models and
vision-language models. vLLM introduces several architectural innovations that dramatically im-
prove inference efficiency while maintaining output quality identical to standard implementations.
Performance Analysis:vLLM achieves remarkable performance improvements over baseline Py-
Torch inference, reducing average latency to 0.896 seconds (a 24.9× speedup) while decreasing mem-
ory consumption to 24.5 GB VRAM (12.8% reduction). This performance profile positions vLLM as
an excellent choice for production deployments requiring rapid response times without extensive
infrastructure modifications.
The latency reduction stems primarily from PagedAttention’s efficient memory management and
continuous batching’s improved GPU utilization. For single-request scenarios common in interac-
tive clinical workflows, the optimized CUDA kernels provide the majority of speedup by reducing
per-token generation time. The memory savings, while modest compared to more aggressive opti-
mization techniques, result from elimination of memory fragmentation and more efficient key-value
cache management.
Deployment Considerations:vLLM deployment is achieved through a simple command-line in-
terface:vllm serve <model_name> –host 0.0.0.0 –port 8000, automatically configuring opti-
mal serving parameters based on detected hardware. The framework exposes an OpenAI-compatible
API, facilitating seamless integration with existing applications designed for GPT-based models.

7.4.3 NVIDIA Triton Inference Server with Python Backend

For enterprise-grade deployments requiring maximum flexibility, multi-model serving, and advanced

learning frameworks, dynamic batching, model ensembles, and comprehensive monitoring capabil-

Architecture and Design Philosophy:Triton employs a plugin-based architecture where different
"backends" handle inference execution for specific frameworks or custom logic. We utilize the Python
backend, enabling integration of our complete preprocessing pipeline, model inference, and post-
processing steps within a unified serving framework. This approach eliminates the need for separate
preprocessing services and reduces network overhead associated with multi-service architectures.
Python Backend Implementation:The Python backend encapsulates our complete inference pipeline
within a Triton-compatible interface. The backend implementation handles:
• Image decoding and preprocessing (normalization, resizing)
• Model initialization and weight loading with optimal memory layout
• Efficient batch inference execution with attention mask generation
• Postprocessing and output formatting
• Error handling and graceful degradation for malformed inputs
Performance Excellence:Triton achieves the best performance among all evaluated serving strate-
gies, delivering an average inference latency of 0.736 seconds per report while consuming only 17.43

over vLLM, coupled with a remarkable 38% reduction in memory footprint compared to the baseline.

7.5 Containerization and Orchestration
To ensure deployment consistency across diverse infrastructure environments and facilitate scalable
production deployments, we containerize all components using Docker with multi-stage builds opti-
mizing image sizes. Base images are built on NVIDIA CUDA containers, ensuring compatibility with
GPU acceleration across different deployment targets.
Health checks and readiness probes ensure traffic is only routed to fully-initialized containers with

Figure 27 illustrates the end-to-end pipeline of the Clara system (Clinical Language Analytics and

The backend pipeline of the Clara system is designed to process chest X-ray inputs efficiently and
prepare them for multi-stage VLM inference. The workflow proceeds as follows:
•Image Preprocessing: Upon receiving an uploaded chest radiograph, the system converts the
byte stream into a processable format and standardizes the resolution to 448×448 pixels. Pa-
tient metadata is validated and injected into predefined prompt templates.
•Turn 1 – Findings Generation: The processed image and structured prompt are forwarded to
the vLLM inference server hosting Qwen2-VL-7B. In this stage, the model focuses on visual
feature extraction and produces a detailed list of radiological findings describing anatomical
structures and abnormalities.
•Turn 2 – Impressions Generation: The findings from Turn 1 are incorporated as contextual
input. A follow-up prompt is issued to elicit diagnostic reasoning, and the model synthesizes
the findings into high-level clinical impressions.
•Response Assembly: Outputs from both inference turns are consolidated into a unified JSON
structure. Latency statistics, token counts, and preprocessing metadata are appended before
returning the final response to the client.
•Result Display: The Gradio frontend renders the findings and impressions, along with pro-
cessing times and prompt details, enabling clinicians to review and assess the generated report
quickly.

9 Conclusion
9.1 Summary of Findings
This project developed and evaluated a Vietnamese Vision–Language AI system for automatic chest
X-ray captioning, focusing on generating clinically meaningfulFindingsandImpressionsto support
radiologists in high-workload environments. Through a two-stage training pipeline, six state-of-the-
art Vision–Language Models (VLMs) were first fine-tuned and compared on a smaller Vietnamese
medical dataset, after which Qwen2-VL-7B [3] emerged as the most suitable backbone across all three
stages: Findings generation, Impressions generation, and multi-turn interaction.
Qwen2-VL-7B [3] was subsequently fine-tuned on a larger dataset of approximately 137k chest X-ray–
report pairs from Military Hospital 175, enabling the model to better capture Vietnamese anatomical,
pathological, and linguistic characteristics. The final system achieved an average inference latency
of about 0.736 seconds per request, indicating that the model can operate at near real-time speeds
compatible with clinical workflows. In addition to automatic metrics, the best-performing model was
further assessed through a doctor benchmark, in which radiologists at Military Hospital 175 qualita-
tively evaluated the clinical usefulness and readability of the generated reports.
9.2 Contributions and Reflections on the Project
The project makes several technical and practical contributions to medical AI in Vietnam. Techni-
cally, it establishes a full pipeline for selecting and fine-tuning Vision–Language Models on domain-
specific Vietnamese chest X-ray data, including a multi-stage training strategy for separate Findings
and Impressions as well as multi-turn vision–language interaction. This pipeline, applied to six com-
petitive VLMs and culminating in a Qwen2-VL-7B [3]–based system, demonstrates that large-scale
Vietnamese medical captioning is feasible with near real-time inference.
From a data and ecosystem perspective, the project leverages a 137k-pair dataset provided by Mil-
itary Hospital 175, ensuring that the model is trained on anatomically and linguistically relevant
Vietnamese clinical data rather than relying solely on Western-centric datasets. Clinically, the sys-
tem is designed as an assistive tool: it can draft detailed Vietnamese Findings and Impressions that
help radiologists reduce workload, improve reporting consistency, and accelerate turnaround time
in settings with radiologist shortages. The collaboration with hospital stakeholders and the doctor
benchmark also provided valuable experience in aligning AI system design with real-world clinical
constraints, balancing model performance, latency, and usability.
9.3 Limitations and Future Work
Despite these achievements, the current system has several limitations, primarily related to data dis-
tribution and external validity. Although the dataset contains around 137k samples, the label distri-
bution is imbalanced: most reports focus on common cardiac and pulmonary conditions, while rare
or complex multi-pathology cases are underrepresented. As a result, the model may perform less
reliably on unusual findings or X-rays that exhibit multiple concurrent abnormalities. Furthermore,
all data originate from a single institution (Military Hospital 175), so model generalization to other
hospitals, imaging devices, and regional populations remains to be systematically evaluated.
Another limitation is that the system mainly operates at the caption level and does not yet inte-
grate explicit visual localization, such as lesion detection or segmentation, which could provide more
transparent, visually grounded explanations for radiologists. In future work, the project aims to (i) ex-
67
pand and diversify the dataset across multiple centers and pathology distributions, and (ii) integrate
detection or segmentation modules to highlight suspicious regions directly on the X-ray, thereby
combining structured visual cues with generated text. Longer-term directions include extending the
framework to other imaging modalities (e.g., CT, MRI, ultrasound), conducting broader clinical val-
idation studies with multiple hospitals, and exploring improved explainability methods for Vision–
Language Models in medical settings.
